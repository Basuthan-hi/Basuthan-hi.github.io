Connective Tissue
Introduction

Connective tissues are main components of the animal body. Under the name of connective there is a wide variety of tissues, but sharing some common features.

characteristic features of connective tissue

  • They are highly vascularised
    It is being supplied with blood vessels.
  • The cells are dispersed in a matrix
    It is a mixture of carbohydrates and proteins
    .
  • The matrix is composed of ground substance criss-crossed by protein fibers.

Function

  • supporting organs and cells
  • transporting nutrients and wastes
  • defending against pathogens
  • storing fat
  • repairing damaged tissues

classification of epithelia

  • Loose connective tissues are present all over the body, where support and elasticity both are needed. Blood vessels, nerves and muscles, all have a loose connective tissue wrapping. They form the subcutaneous layer under the skin along with adipose tissues, attaching muscles and other structures to the skin.

    • Areolar Tissue: It is present under the skin and supports epithelium. It contains randomly distributed fibres, fibroblasts, mast cells and macrophages. It supports the organs present in the abdominal cavity, fills the space between muscle fibres and wraps around blood and lymph vessels.

      location

      areolar tissue is present in the dermis of the skin and in areas between muscle bundles, contributing to the overall structural integrity and elasticity of these regions. Its loose, mesh-like composition allows for the efficient exchange of nutrients and waste products, making it a vital component in maintaining healthy tissue function.

      function

      Areolar tissue plays a crucial role in the body by providing support and flexibility to various structures. Additionally, areolar tissue contains immune cells that help defend against pathogens, contributing to the bodyâ€™s overall immune response. Its ability to act as a fluid reservoir helps maintain tissue hydration and electrolyte balance, making it an essential component in maintaining healthy tissue function.

    • Adipose Tissue: They are present under the skin and store fat. It acts as a shock absorber and helps in maintaining body temperature in colder environments.

      • White Adipose Tissues(WAT) protect kidneys and are also found at the back of the eye, in the hump of camels, blubber of whales, etc.

      • Brown Adipose Tissue(BAT) is found in infants, polar bears, penguins and other animals found in cold regions. It contains more mitochondria and generates 20 times more heat as compared to the other fat. It releases metabolic heat.

      location

      It is found beneath the skin, where it acts as subcutaneous fat, providing insulation and helping to regulate body temperature. Around internal organs, such as the liver, kidneys, and intestines, adipose tissue forms visceral fat, which cushions and protects these vital structures. Additionally, it is present in bone marrow as yellow bone marrow, between muscles as intermuscular fat, and within breast tissue.

      function

      Adipose tissue, commonly known as fat tissue, serves several vital functions in the body: Energy Storage: It stores energy in the form of lipids, which can be used by the body when needed. Insulation: It helps to insulate the body, maintaining body temperature by providing a layer of thermal insulation. Cushioning and Protection: It cushions and protects internal organs, reducing the risk of injury. Hormone Production: It produces hormones such as leptin, which regulates appetite and metabolism.     • Reticular Connective Tissue: It is made up of reticular fibres. It supports the internal framework of organs such as liver, lymph nodes and spleen.

  • In the dense connective tissue, fibroblast cells and fibres are compactly packed. Their main function is to support and transmit mechanical forces. They are somewhat less flexible than loose connective tissue. On the basis of the arrangement of collagen fibres, they are divided into two types

    • Dense regular tissue: In the dense regular connective tissue, the orientation of fibres are regular. The collagen fibres are present between the parallel running bundles of fibres. The regular arrangement enhances tensile strength and poses resistance to stretching in the direction of the orientation of fibre. Examples of dense regular tissue are tendons and ligaments.

    • Dense irregular tissue: There are many fibres including collagen, which are oriented irregularly or randomly. The irregular arrangement gives uniform strength in all directions. Fibres may form a mesh-like network. This type of tissue is present in the dermis of the skin.

    • Elastic connective tissue is a type of connective tissue that contains a high proportion of elastic fibers. These fibers allow the tissue to stretch and recoil, providing flexibility and resilience.
